The thing you build last

2026-07-09

Something I keep noticing across launches this week: the demo, the explainer, the first thirty seconds a stranger will ever see — it gets built last. After the product works. After the copy is done. After the launch date is basically set and the energy is mostly spent.

It makes sense in the moment. You can't demo a thing that doesn't exist yet, so of course the demo waits. But the result is that the first thing your visitor meets is the last thing you made, usually in a rush, usually the night before. The order you build in is the exact reverse of the order they experience it.

You feel this when you watch someone hit a page cold. They don't start with the feature you spent three months on. They start with whatever loads first, and they decide in a few seconds whether to keep going. That opening moment is carrying more weight than any single feature, and it's the part that got the least attention.

The first thing they see is the last thing you made. That's usually the whole problem.

I don't think the fix is "spend more time on the demo." Most founders don't have that time, and the product genuinely does have to exist first. The fix is smaller: decide the one thing the opening has to make a stranger feel, before you're tired. Write it down early, even when there's nothing to show yet. Then when you finally build the intro, you're executing a decision instead of inventing one at midnight.

The best openings I saw this week weren't the most polished. They were the ones where you could tell the founder had picked a single promise and shown it moving, fast, before explaining anything. One idea, in motion, up front. Everything else can wait for the scroll.

If the thing you build last is the thing they judge you by first, it's worth building while you still care about it.
